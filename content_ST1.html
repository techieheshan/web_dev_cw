Worth knowing Passing an exam and being able to do the job it's supposed to lead to have never been the same test — and employers are increasingly the ones pointing that out, not just students.

From Passing Grades to Real Skills

Sri Lanka's O/Level and A/Level system produces genuinely strong exam results. What it doesn't always produce, on its own, is someone ready to walk into a workplace and be useful on day one. This page is about that gap — why it exists, and what's actually within a student's control to do about it before graduation, not after.

The Exam Treadmill

"Grades measure how well you performed on one specific day. They were never designed to measure whether you can do the job."

Years of exam preparation build real discipline — but discipline alone isn't the same as job-readiness.

Sri Lanka's education system is, by regional standards, exam-heavy: results on the O/Level and A/Level largely determine which stream, which university place, and which future a student is funnelled toward. That system rewards a specific, narrow skill extremely well — recalling and reproducing material under timed pressure. It's a real skill, and it's not nothing. But it's also not the same skill as solving a problem that doesn't have a mark scheme, working in a team, or explaining an idea to someone who disagrees with you.

The treadmill effect is that the better a student gets at the exam game, the less time and incentive they have left to practise anything else — right up until they graduate and discover the workplace is testing for a different set of skills entirely.

The Gap Employers Actually See

A strong transcript gets you the interview. It rarely wins it on its own.

Talk to anyone who's actually hired a recent graduate in Sri Lanka and a pattern comes up fast: candidates with excellent grades who still struggle with basic workplace communication, who've never had to manage their own project deadline without a teacher chasing them, or who've genuinely never used the specific software tools the job actually requires. None of that is a failure of intelligence. It's a gap between what the curriculum tests and what the job tests.

It's worth being precise about what's actually missing, because "soft skills" gets used as a vague catch-all. In practice it usually means: being able to work without being told exactly what to do next, being comfortable saying "I don't know, let me find out," and having built something — anything — outside of an exam hall that another person can actually look at.

What's Slowly Changing

Apprenticeship-style mentoring teaches things a classroom structurally can't.

Vocational training centres and apprenticeship-style programmes have been quietly growing as an alternative track — places where the entire point is doing the task under someone experienced, not writing about the task in an exam booklet. Student-run coding clubs, hackathons, and skill-share workshops on university campuses are doing something similar informally: giving students a reason to build something real, with a deadline that isn't an exam.

Peer-led skill-shares let students teach each other things the syllabus doesn't cover.

None of this requires an institution to organise it, either. A small group of students teaching each other practical skills — one person who's good at design teaching two others, in exchange for help with something they're weak in — covers ground a formal curriculum won't get to for years, if ever.

What You Can Do Before You Graduate

The earlier you start, the less it costs you later. None of this requires waiting for a module to teach it:

  • Build one thing nobody assigned you. A small project, a write-up, a tool — something an employer can actually look at that isn't a transcript.
  • Ask for the unglamorous tasks. Volunteering for the boring parts of a group project (coordinating, documenting, following up) is where the actually transferable skills live.
  • Teach a peer a skill you already have. Explaining something out loud exposes the gaps in your own understanding faster than any exam does.
  • Say yes to one thing outside your comfort subject. A short workshop, a club, a one-off task in an unfamiliar area — the discomfort is the point.

The Outcome

This isn't an argument that grades don't matter — they open doors, and pretending otherwise would be dishonest. It's an argument that grades alone are an incomplete bet. The students who end up genuinely prepared are usually the ones who treated their exam results as the minimum requirement, not the finish line, and spent the time everyone else spent on one more past paper building something a mark scheme was never going to measure.
